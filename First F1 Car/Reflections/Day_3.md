## 🛠️ Skills & Techniques Learned
- `Combining Bodies` — Merging independent solid bodies after completing individual operations to enable features such as fillets, sketches, and cuts across multiple components.
- `Applying Prior Knowledge` — Reinforcing previously learned concepts by recognizing when they can be applied to solve new modeling problems.

## 💭 Reflection
Today's session was `particularly rewarding and smooth`. I was able to jump straight into both the modeling and the recording process without the lengthy preparation that I experienced during the first few days of the project. It's surprising how something that initially felt complicated and time-consuming has now become second nature after a few sessions and establishing a solid workflow. Looking back, I'm `glad I invested the time to build that foundation` because it has allowed me to `spend much more time actually designing and learning`.

Today's focus was on the `center section of the Formula One car`. I began by modeling the engine intake, one of the highest and most recognizable components of the car. Creating this feature once again involved the Loft command using multiple profile sketches and guide curves. Compared to Day 1 and Day 2, `using the loft feature felt incredibly natural`. When I first encountered lofting, I struggled to understand what the feature was actually doing and found it difficult to visualize how the profiles and guide curves came together to form a smooth three-dimensional shape. However, after several opportunities to practice and a much better understanding gained from Day 2, creating the engine intake felt almost effortless. It was satisfying to see how much `my confidence with the feature had grown in such a short time`.

The only notable challenge I encountered today was, interestingly, the opposite of a problem I had solved on Day 2. Previously, I learned the importance of creating separate solid bodies so that individual components could be modified independently. This concept became essential when I needed to apply cuts to the side extension without affecting the main chassis. Today, however, the challenge involved `combining bodies` instead. While applying fillets around the cockpit, I noticed that I could successfully fillet edges within the main chassis but not the edges connecting the cockpit to the chassis itself. After investigating the issue, I realized that `the cockpit and chassis still existed as two separate solid bodies`. Because of this, SolidWorks treated them as independent parts and could not create a continuous fillet across their shared boundary. Although it took some time to identify the cause, I `eventually recognized that the issue was directly related to the body assignment concepts I had learned earlier in the project`. Rather than immediately searching for an answer, I `relied on my previous understanding` of how SolidWorks handles separate bodies and concluded that combining the two bodies would likely resolve the problem. After merging them into a single solid body, the fillet worked exactly as intended. What made this experience particularly meaningful was not the solution itself, but the reasoning behind it. Instead of following another tutorial, `I applied knowledge from a previous challenge to solve a completely different problem`. It reinforced the idea that `truly understanding a concept is far more valuable than simply memorizing a sequence of steps`. A strong conceptual understanding allows the same knowledge to be `adapted creatively whenever new challenges arise`.

Apart from the cockpit fillet, the remainder of today's modeling session was relatively straightforward. The T-camera and shoulder camera mainly involved fundamental sketching and extrusion techniques, making them enjoyable to model without requiring extensive troubleshooting.

I also made `my first significant design decision` during this project by changing the appearance of the model from `white to gray`. Although gray is not the final color I plan to use, it provides `much better visual contrast than the default white appearance`. The darker shading makes it easier to `distinguish surfaces, evaluate the curvature of the bodywork, and spot small imperfections` that would otherwise be difficult to notice. It was a simple change, but one that noticeably improved my overall modeling experience.

Overall, today's session `highlighted how much progress I've already made since beginning this project`. Tasks that once felt intimidating are becoming increasingly intuitive, and concepts that once seemed unrelated are now connecting naturally. More importantly, I'm beginning to `rely less on tutorials and more on my own understanding` to solve problems. That shift in mindset has been one of the most rewarding outcomes of this project so far, and it `gives me confidence as I move on to the more complex stages of designing the Formula One car`.
