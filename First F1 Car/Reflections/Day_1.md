## 💭 Reflection
<p> This was the first day of the project, and getting started turned out to be much more challenging than I had expected. 
Before even opening SolidWorks, I spent a considerable amount of time <code>researching different Formula One modeling tutorials</code> to find an approach that best matched my learning goals. 
Many tutorials relied heavily on <code>sketching over reference images</code>, but I discovered that the version of SolidWorks I'm currently using has a transparency issue that makes image tracing much less practical. 
Because of this limitation, I had to rethink how I approached the project from the very beginning. At one point during the preparation phase, I even considered switching to modeling a Porsche 911 instead. 
Compared to a Formula One car, it felt like a much more manageable first project. However, after thinking about my long-term goal of becoming a motorsports engineer, I decided to <code>embrace the challenge</code> rather than avoid it. 
If this project is meant to prepare me for increasingly complex CAD work, then there was no better place to start than with one of the most aerodynamically sophisticated vehicles in motorsport. <br>
  
Another challenge was `documenting the project` itself. I wanted to record my entire modeling process, not only to create timelapse videos, but also to `demonstrate that every part of the model was built by me.` 
Since the project is guided by existing tutorials, I felt it was important to clearly show my own work and learning process instead of simply presenting the final result.
Thus, recording the process introduced its own `technical difficulties`. My laptop struggled to run SolidWorks, a tutorial video, screen recording software, dual displays, and a camera recording me at the same time. 
There were several rounds of `trial and error` before I found a workflow that allowed me to document everything without causing SolidWorks to become unbearably slow. 
Although it took time to set up, I'm glad I invested the effort because I now have a workflow that I can continue using throughout the rest of the project. <br>

`The modeling process itself was also more difficult than I anticipated.` Rather than blindly following the tutorial, I `wanted to understand the reasoning` behind each feature and experiment with my own ideas whenever possible. 
Initially, I `tried modifying` different curves and body shapes, but I quickly realized that changing the main body too early would make it `difficult to maintain accurate dimensions` for the rest of the car. 
Because so many future components depend on the chassis geometry, I decided that the smarter approach would be to first `learn the techniques and complete the foundational structure` before introducing my own design ideas later in the project. 
This approach allows me to build a strong understanding of the workflow without compromising the overall model. The most challenging new technique I encountered today was `Loft`. 
At first, I struggled to visualize how multiple sketch profiles and guide curves combined to create smooth surfaces. It felt unintuitive compared to the more straightforward features I had used previously. 
After experimenting with different sketches and following the tutorial carefully, I eventually understood the basic concept, but I know there's still plenty of room for improvement. 
I'll be watching additional tutorials and practicing with simpler examples to strengthen my understanding before relying on lofts more heavily in future designs. <br>

Looking back, this first day involved `far more preparation, troubleshooting, and learning` than actual modeling. While it was slower than I originally expected, every obstacle forced me to improve either my workflow, my understanding of SolidWorks, or my overall approach to engineering projects. 
Although there is still a long journey ahead, I `feel much more confident` after overcoming these initial challenges. With the groundwork now in place, I hope to build momentum and tackle the more complex components of the Formula One car in the coming sessions. </p>
